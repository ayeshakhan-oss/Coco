Candidate Feedback Emails


Training Guide for People & Culture Team


















Hi team,


I'm sending this on behalf of Haroon to help you understand how we could structure our feedback emails better. This document walks through the HOG candidate emails as teaching examples, with annotations explaining the principles behind each choice.


The goal: make every candidate feel 
seen
, not just evaluated.
























"Your prompt is to tell them all of this with love and in detail, in specificity."


— Haroon Yasin, January 29, 2026


















Communication Principles








1. Lead with the result
 — First sentence = the answer. No anxiety.












2. Use "we" not "I"
 — Company voice. "We were worried" not "I was worried"












3. Be extremely specific
 — "At 47 minutes, you said..." not "Your insight was great"












4. Show company vulnerability
 — "By the third interview, we were worried..."












5. The P.S. that lands
 — The thing they'll screenshot and send to their spouse


































Email 1: Waqas Tanveer


SELECTED AS HEAD OF GROWTH




















Subject: 
You're in, Waqas.












Waqas,


You're our Head of Growth.










💡 WHY THIS WORKS:
 First sentence = the answer. Like telling a friend, not issuing a corporate memo.








Something we don't usually share: by the third interview, we were worried. Smart candidates, sure. But something was missing.


Then you walked in with a shoulder injury you didn't mention until halfway through. Someone finally asked why you kept shifting. You could have rescheduled. You didn't.










💡 COMPANY VULNERABILITY:
 "We were worried" = the company is human. Noticing the shoulder detail = we were paying attention. Most candidates never know what interviewers were feeling.








About 47 minutes in, we challenged your pilot size. You'd said 300-500 teachers. We pushed back with data suggesting 50.


You paused. Then you said something the room wrote down:










"I was trying to fit the data into my interpretation, whereas my interpretation should have fit into the data."








We've interviewed hundreds of people. We can count on one hand how many have caught themselves mid-reasoning and named the bias in real time. That kind of self-correction wins.










💡 "THE ROOM WROTE IT DOWN":
 Collective witness, not individual opinion. "47 minutes in" = we remember the actual moment.
















Since we're going to work together, here's what we'll work on:


The small stuff will bite you.
 You didn't convert UTC to local time. In a government contract, that kind of miss becomes a deadline problem. We'll build checklists together. Your brain is on the big picture; we'll help with the details.


Strong instincts need verification partners.
 That 300-500 teacher number came from gut feel. The data said 50. We want the instincts. We'll pair them with a check step.










💡 FEEDBACK AS PARTNERSHIP:
 "We'll build checklists together" = shared work, not criticism.
















What happens now:


Jawwad from People & Culture will send the formal offer. Start date, compensation, the details. We wanted you to hear from us first.


We know you're weighing options. Your wife is in the US. There's a green card process. Life is complicated. Whatever you decide: we see the whole person. If you come, we'll figure the rest out together.




The People & Culture Team
Taleemabad










P.S. — The "value discovery constraint" phrase you used? It's showing up in board meetings now. You're already shaping how we think.
















💡 THE P.S. THAT LANDS:
 Unexpected. Shows influence before they've even joined. This is what makes someone screenshot and send to their spouse.
























Email 2: Zeest Qureshi


SELECTED AS DEPUTY HEAD OF GROWTH
















Subject: 
2019, 2020, 2026.


















💡 SUBJECT LINE THAT TELLS A STORY:
 She emailed in 2019. Again in 2020. Now it's 2026. Three numbers. Seven years. She'll know what this means.








Zeest,


You're in.


Deputy Head of Growth. Reporting to Waqas. We're building this role around you becoming Head of Growth when the time comes.


Something we need to say first.


When you mentioned that you'd reached out to Taleemabad in 2019, we made a note. Then you said you tried again in 2020. The room went quiet.


Seven years. You've been circling back to this for seven years.


We don't know what happened in 2019 that made us miss you. We don't know why 2020 didn't work out. But most people move on. They find other jobs, other missions. You kept coming back.


That kind of persistence is identity.










💡 HONOR THEIR JOURNEY:
 "The room went quiet" = collective witness. "That kind of persistence is identity" = simple statement, not the AI pattern "isn't enthusiasm. It's identity."
















What convinced us:


You were the only candidate who invented vocabulary. "Effective user." "Pedagogical user." Everyone else used our terms. You created your own. They were better.


And then the moment we tested you. We pushed back on institutional accountability, called it temporary, performative. Most candidates would have agreed with us or defended their original point.


You said:








"Then call it what it is."








And you rebuilt your argument from that new foundation. In real time. In front of five people. We can't train that.










💡 SHOW THEM THEIR OWN BRILLIANCE:
 Most people don't realize their best moments. "We can't train that" = simple, direct praise.
















The honest part.


Your market entry section was thin. Punjab, Balochistan. You said your "brain was tired." We believe you. But this role won't have the luxury of tired. We'll give you templates. Waqas will mentor you on B2G for the first few months. The stamina is yours to build.


Also: your SQL had a redundancy. 
lifespan_days > 0
 was already implied by 
active_days > 1
. Small thing. But it shows a pattern of thoroughness over elegance. Worth watching.










What happens now:


You mentioned 30 days for notice. That's fine. Leave well.


You also mentioned Karachi. We're going to help with the move. Housing support, logistics. That's reserved for people we believe in.


Zeest? We believe in you.


Jawwad will send the details. We wanted you to hear from us first:


Welcome home. Finally.










💡 "WELCOME HOME. FINALLY.":
 Two words. Seven years of waiting. This is the line that lands.








The People & Culture Team
Taleemabad








P.S. — We found your 2020 email. You wrote, "I believe I could contribute to Taleemabad's mission." You were right. We just had to catch up.














💡 THE P.S. THAT REWRITES HISTORY:
 We went back and found her old email. "We just had to catch up" = company takes responsibility. This is what she screenshots and sends to her parents.
























Email 3: Waleed Bin Zahid


NOT SELECTED — KEEP WARM
















Subject: 
Not this role. But we want to stay in touch.
















💡 SUBJECT LINE:
 "Not this role" = clear decision, no anxiety. "We want to stay in touch" = this isn't goodbye.








Waleed,


This isn't a yes.


You came third out of seven. You outperformed four other candidates, including some with impressive backgrounds. For Head of Growth, specifically, we're going another direction.


Here's why we're writing anyway.








There was a moment in your interview that stuck with us.


You said something about career intentionality:








"Some people are in desperation and they need a job. Some people take career twice. Where they are not very unhappy in their current role."








Someone who knows their worth. Someone evaluating us as much as we're evaluating them.


That landed with us.


Your B2G relationships are real. PEF, PECTA, PIMA. You've walked into those offices, built those relationships, navigated that bureaucracy. Most people have "contacts." You have contracts.








What held you back?


We'll be specific because you deserve specificity.


The AI filtered your data without you noticing.
 You reported 275 sessions. The actual number was 604. Somewhere, the AI applied a completion filter you didn't ask for. You submitted it without catching it. For this role, that matters.


Some B2G relationships were less warm than initially presented.
 When we probed, the depth wasn't quite there. Not lying. Optimism. But precision matters when you're building government partnerships.


For Head of Growth specifically:
 we needed more evidence of framework creation, not execution. You're strong at running plays. We needed someone who invents them.








💡 SPECIFIC FEEDBACK WITHOUT CRUELTY:
 "Not lying. Optimism." = short, not softened.














Where we want to leave things.


You mentioned interest in Product roles. Your execution orientation in government contexts could be a superpower there.


If we open something in that direction, we'd like to reach out. Not as a courtesy. Because we think there's a fit somewhere.


Would you be open to staying connected?








💡 KEEP WARM = SPECIFIC:
 Don't say "we'll keep you in mind" (meaningless). "Would you be open to staying connected?" = invites response.








The People & Culture Team
Taleemabad








P.S. — The 71 minutes you gave us, plus the case study work. It wasn't wasted. You helped us understand what we're looking for. That's a contribution.

Soul Architect / Conversational UX Designer

Dear Talal,

We have completed our review of your case study submission alongside the
conversation you had with Jawwad on March 12. We are writing to let you
know that we will not be moving you forward for the Soul Architect role
specifically. But we want to be equally clear about something else: we want
to stay in touch, and we want to explain exactly why.

What We Liked Most About You

The crypto campaign story stopped the room. A junior colleague had sent a
client presentation without warning you, and instead of escalating,
distancing yourself, or letting it land badly, you stepped in, handled the
client yourself, and only then had a direct conversation with your
colleague privately. That instinct, to protect someone publicly and teach
them privately, is rarer than most people recognise. It told us something
genuine about how you understand what it means to back a teammate.

The teacher feedback story was equally striking. You were a student. The
person you were challenging held formal authority over your academic
standing. And yet when you saw a senior teacher using punishment and
disengagement as classroom management, you chose to have the conversation
anyway, respectfully and directly, and it worked. She changed her approach.
That kind of courage does not come from training. It comes from a
particular view of what is right.

And then there was the Pakistan national anthem. You mentioned that you
open your meetings with it. It is a small detail, but it told us something
about how you carry joy through your work. Quirky, personal, and entirely
your own. We smiled when you said it. We hold on to moments like these.
They are the reason this is a warm bench decision, not a closed door.

Where We Found Ourselves Sitting With Questions

The case study asked you to work through four specific datasets and show us
how you think at the level of data. That is where the gap became visible.
The most significant issue was your interpretation of Teacher E. In your
submission, you wrote that Teacher E engages with Rumi in a more
collaborative and reflective manner, asking follow-up questions and
exploring teaching strategies, and functions as a co-teacher or
instructional coach. When we sat with the actual data, we found the
opposite. Teacher E is the most self-sufficient, boundary-aware teacher in
the dataset. Her most defining interaction with Rumi is essentially this:
when I feel I need assistance, I will ask for it. That is not a
collaborative relationship. That is a confident, autonomous one.

This matters not because of the label itself, but because Teacher E is a
load-bearing figure in understanding the Sri Lanka usage pattern. There is
a structural explanation rooted in how specific institutions credential
their teachers, and it surfaces clearly when you follow Teacher E's data
carefully. Because that character was misread, the Sri Lanka analysis
remained at the level of general cultural observation rather than specific
diagnosis. The two are connected in a way that is hard to see without
sitting inside the data.

The data section showed a similar pattern. You identified real themes,
teacher talk time dominance, emotional dependency signals, progress
tracking behaviour. These are not wrong observations. But they are the kind
of observations that anyone who read the dataset description could arrive
at. What we were looking for was evidence that you had been inside the data
itself, a specific teacher ID with exact metrics tracked across sessions, a
cross-dataset connection found by linking files that were not obviously
related, a moment where the numbers told you something that the description
did not. That level of specificity was not present, and in this role, it is
the difference between describing the surface and reading the room.

What We Think You Should Do Next

You have genuine instincts, and the values you showed us are not things we
find easily. So we want to say this directly: we are not closing the
relationship. We are keeping your profile active and will reach out when a
role opens that is a better match for where you are right now. In the
meantime, the one thing that will move you fastest in this space is
practising analytical precision. Take any dataset you care about and force
yourself to go two levels deeper than your first observation. Do not write
the insight until you can name the specific row, session, or number that
made you think it. Do that consistently and you will surprise yourself with
what starts to surface.

The AI fluency piece sits alongside this. Moving beyond using AI to confirm
what you already see, and starting to use it to challenge your first
conclusion, to argue against your hypothesis, to find what your analysis
might have missed, that is the iterative exchange this kind of work runs
on. The gap between someone who uses AI and someone who thinks with it is
exactly this, and it is a gap that closes quickly with deliberate practice.

We are rooting for you, Talal. Stay close.

P.S.

The Pakistan anthem story stayed with us. Not because it is unusual, but
because it is specific. It is a small window into someone who has actually
thought about what they want their work to feel like, not just what they
want it to produce. That quality will serve you well.

Warm regards,

People and Culture Team

Taleemabad
